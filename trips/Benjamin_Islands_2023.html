North Channel • Ontario

Benjamin Islands

August 7–10, 2023

Chapter 1

Planning the Expedition

The Benjamin Islands had been quietly sitting in the back of my mind for over a year.

I had heard them mentioned only a few times, but every mention was from someone who know something about special places. Every mention was a hint, that this wasn't just another destination to kayak to, it was a hidden gem.

At the time, I was still cautious about committing to long solo expeditions in unfamiliar waters. Paddling alone always carries a certain amount of risk, but unknown coastlines introduce another layer of uncertainty.

My biggest concern was never the paddling itself.

It was knowing whether I'd actually find a safe place to end the day. A suitable campsite needs far more than a flat surface for a tent, or trees to support a hammock. It needs enough shelter if the weather gets bad, trees nearby to hang food safely away from wildlife, and a shoreline where I could unload a fully loaded sea kayak without fighting waves or cliffs.

Studying the Unknown

Most of the expedition happened long before my kayak ever touched the water.

Countless evenings disappeared into Google Maps. I measured crossings, estimated paddling distances, searched for potential campsites, and tried to imagine how exposed each section of coastline would become if the weather changed.

Surprisingly little had been written about paddling the Benjamin Islands, making every decision feel like an educated guess. Looking at their location on the map, I assumed the islands would simply be more of the same. Smooth pink granite but fewer islands. Beautiful, certainly, but nothing radically different from what I had already experienced in the Georgian Bay.

I couldn't have been more wrong.

The Final Push

The trip became impossible to ignore after coming across a well hidden YouTube video

The North Channel was described as one of Ontario's undiscovered paddling locations. David, a kayak guide, mentions how many people visit the Eastern Georgian Bay, due to it's proximity to Toronto. But the landscape gets more remote and scenic as you continue North.

“People tend to stop at Killarney, but keep going. It's worth the extra hour drive”

Still reluctant to commit to this journey with only weeks leading up to my vacation, another unexpected source of inspiration appeared. Nancy Yanaky, whose Group of Seven inspired paintings I had admired in Killarney, shared one inspired by the Benjamin Islands.

That was the moment the trip stopped being an idea, and crystalized into a plan.

Chapter 2

The Journey North

August 6, 2023

I left Montreal and made the long drive west toward Spanish, Ontario. That evening I checked into a small motel nearby, hoping for an early start.

Like many of the trips I make to Northern Ontario, it didn't take long for me to make a new friend. He introduced himself as

Benjamin, a miner from Northern Ontario.

When I mentioned that I was planning to paddle to the Benjamin Islands the next morning, his face immediately lit up. He told me he had visited the islands years earlier and remembered them vividly. His description wasn't about beaches or campsites or sunsets. Instead, he smiled and simply said:

"There's some highly metamorphosed rock there."

At the time, I wasn't entirely sure what he meant. I was about to find out.

Chapter 03

Into the North Channel

August 7, 2023

The rain finally eased just before noon. After months of planning, studying satellite imagery and wondering what waited beyond the Spanish River, it was finally time to launch.

The dockmaster at Spanish Marina pointed me toward the Benjamin Islands with only a few brief instructions. I packed the last of my gear, pushed away from the dock, and began the long paddle east. Within minutes the marina disappeared behind me. For the first time that day, I was truly alone.

The shoreline was rugged. It offered no exit points for a paddler that requires frequents breaks. My first break came at Little Detroit Cut, a small passage that connects the Spanish River to the North Channel. I settled for a sheltered spot in ankle high water. The quartz running through the surrounding rock hinted that this landscape was already beginning to change.

Leaving Little Detroit Cut felt like crossing an invisible boundary. The dark, river suddenly transformed into something entirely different. The water changed colour. The swell became confused. For twenty or thirty metres the kayak rode through a strange, rolling turbulence before emerging into the North Channel. Ahead lay Dreamer's Bay. Beyond that... The Benjamin Islands.

"Everything beyond this point was completely unknown."
